METRIC: Burn Rate & Runway
CATEGORY: Liquidity

DEFINITION
Burn rate is how much cash a company spends per month in excess of
what it earns. Runway is how many months the company can continue
operating at its current burn rate before running out of cash.
Together they are the most urgent liquidity metrics — they tell
you not just whether the company is struggling, but how much time
it has to fix the problem. A company can be unprofitable for years
if it has sufficient runway. A company with no runway has days.

HOW IT'S CALCULATED
Monthly Burn Rate = Total Monthly Expenses - Total Monthly Revenue
(only meaningful when expenses exceed revenue; if revenue exceeds
expenses the company has a positive cash flow, not a burn rate)

Net Burn Rate = Cash spent minus cash received (cash basis)
Gross Burn Rate = Total cash spent regardless of revenue

Runway (months) = Current Cash Balance / Monthly Burn Rate

Example: Cash balance of $400,000, monthly burn of $45,000
= 8.9 months of runway.

BENCHMARKS — PROFESSIONAL SERVICES INDUSTRY
Runway:
  Safe:        18 months or more
  Adequate:    12 to 18 months
  Warning:     6 to 12 months
  Critical:    Under 6 months
  Emergency:   Under 3 months

Monthly Burn Rate (relative to revenue):
  Healthy:     Positive cash flow (no burn)
  Acceptable:  Burn under 10% of monthly revenue
  Warning:     Burn 10% to 25% of monthly revenue
  Critical:    Burn above 25% of monthly revenue

WHAT IT TELLS THE ADVISER
- Whether the company can survive without external intervention
- How much time leadership has to execute a turnaround
- Whether the current strategy is financially sustainable
- How urgently cost reduction or revenue acceleration is needed

WARNING SIGNS
- Runway under 12 months with no clear path to profitability
- Burn rate increasing month over month (the hole is getting deeper)
- Cash balance declining faster than the P&L suggests
  (hidden cash drains: prepayments, debt service, owner distributions)
- Runway sufficient but burn accelerating — the clock is moving faster
- Company aware of the runway problem but not acting on it

CONNECTIONS TO OTHER METRICS
- High burn + high DSO = company is losing cash AND its receivables
  are slow — a compounding liquidity crisis
- High burn + declining revenue = runway is shorter than it appears
  because revenue will continue falling
- High burn + high expenses in one category = expense breakdown
  will identify the specific cost driver
- High burn + healthy profit margin = unusual; check whether the
  margin calculation excludes a major cash cost

SAMPLE INTERPRETATION
A services firm has a cash balance of $520,000 and monthly expenses
of $98,000 against monthly revenue of $71,000. Net burn is $27,000
per month. Runway is 19.3 months. Despite the burn, this is in the
Adequate range — the company has time to act. The adviser should
note that runway assumes stable revenue. If revenue continues its
current decline, recalculate runway using projected revenue, which
will shorten the timeline materially. Immediate priority: identify
whether the burn is driven by fixed costs (structural problem requiring
headcount decisions) or variable costs (controllable with discipline).
