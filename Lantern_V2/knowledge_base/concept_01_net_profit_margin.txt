METRIC: Net Profit Margin
CATEGORY: Profitability

DEFINITION
Net profit margin measures how much of every dollar in revenue a company
actually keeps as profit after all expenses — including operating costs,
salaries, taxes, and overhead — have been paid. It is the single most
important indicator of a company's overall financial health and pricing power.

HOW IT'S CALCULATED
Net Profit Margin = (Net Income / Total Revenue) × 100
Net Income = Total Revenue minus Total Expenses
Example: Revenue of $500,000 and expenses of $350,000 produces a
net income of $150,000 and a margin of 30%.

BENCHMARKS — PROFESSIONAL SERVICES INDUSTRY
Excellent:  30% and above
Healthy:    20% to 29%
Adequate:   10% to 19%
Warning:    5% to 9%
Critical:   Below 5% or negative

WHAT IT TELLS THE ADVISER
- Whether the company is pricing its services appropriately
- Whether cost structure is sustainable relative to revenue
- Whether the business model is fundamentally viable
- How much buffer exists to absorb revenue shocks or unexpected costs

WARNING SIGNS
- Margin declining over consecutive periods even while revenue grows
  (costs are scaling faster than revenue — a structural problem)
- Margin below 10% in a services firm with no capital expenditure
  (labor and overhead costs are out of control)
- Negative margin — the company is actively destroying cash
- Margin significantly below industry peers suggests underpricing
  or bloated headcount

CONNECTIONS TO OTHER METRICS
- Low margin + high burn rate = immediate liquidity crisis risk
- Low margin + rising DSO = double pressure: not profitable AND
  not collecting what it earns
- Low margin + high revenue per employee = pricing problem,
  not a productivity problem
- Low margin + rapid revenue decline = existential threat

SAMPLE INTERPRETATION
A services firm reports net profit margin of 8.3%. This falls in the
Warning range. While the company is technically profitable, an 8.3%
margin provides minimal buffer. A 10% revenue decline would push it
near breakeven. The adviser should investigate whether this reflects
a temporary cost spike (acceptable) or a structural pricing problem
(requires intervention). Cross-reference with expense breakdown to
identify the largest cost driver.
