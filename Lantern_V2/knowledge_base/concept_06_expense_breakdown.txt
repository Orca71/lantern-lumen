METRIC: Expense Breakdown
CATEGORY: Profitability

DEFINITION
Expense breakdown disaggregates total costs into categories to reveal
where money is actually going. In professional services, expenses
tend to cluster into a small number of categories — compensation,
facilities, technology, marketing, and administration. Understanding
the composition of expenses is essential for targeted cost management:
you cannot reduce what you cannot see. Expense breakdown also
distinguishes between recurring costs (fixed obligations that persist
regardless of revenue) and one-time costs (controllable and temporary).

HOW IT'S CALCULATED
Category Percentage = (Category Total / Grand Total Expenses) × 100

Recurring Ratio = (Recurring Expenses / Total Expenses) × 100

A high recurring ratio means the cost structure is largely fixed —
dangerous in a declining revenue environment because costs cannot
be easily reduced.

BENCHMARKS — PROFESSIONAL SERVICES INDUSTRY
Typical expense composition for a healthy services firm:
  Compensation (salaries, benefits):  55% to 65% of total expenses
  Facilities & occupancy:             8% to 12%
  Technology & software:              5% to 10%
  Marketing & business development:   5% to 10%
  General & administrative:           8% to 15%

Single category concentration:
  Acceptable:   Any single category under 40%
  Concerning:   Any single category 40% to 55%
  Dangerous:    Any single category above 55%

Recurring expense ratio:
  Healthy:   Under 60% recurring
  Warning:   60% to 75% recurring
  Critical:  Above 75% recurring (highly inflexible cost structure)

WHAT IT TELLS THE ADVISER
- Which cost categories have the most reduction potential
- Whether the cost structure is flexible (variable) or rigid (fixed)
- Whether any single expense category is disproportionately large
- Whether the company is investing appropriately in growth
  (marketing) vs. running lean (administrative)

WARNING SIGNS
- Compensation above 70% of total expenses with declining revenue
  (headcount is too large for the revenue base — layoffs likely needed)
- Facilities costs high relative to headcount
  (underutilized office space — a legacy cost from a larger operation)
- Technology costs growing rapidly without corresponding revenue growth
  (tool sprawl or failed technology investments)
- Marketing near zero in a company with declining revenue
  (not investing in the growth needed to reverse the trend)
- Recurring ratio above 75% — the company has almost no ability
  to cut costs quickly if revenue falls further

CONNECTIONS TO OTHER METRICS
- High compensation % + low revenue per employee = overstaffed
  relative to productivity
- High recurring expenses + declining revenue + low runway =
  the most dangerous combination; fixed costs will exhaust cash
- High expenses in one category + healthy margin overall = that
  category is being offset by strong revenue; watch if revenue dips
- Expense breakdown should always be read alongside burn rate
  to understand which categories are driving the cash drain

SAMPLE INTERPRETATION
A services firm shows compensation at 71% of total expenses, with
a recurring ratio of 79%. Revenue has declined 17% year over year.
The adviser should flag this as a structural cost problem. The company
has a largely fixed cost base dominated by labor, but its revenue is
shrinking. It cannot cut costs quickly. Unless revenue recovers,
headcount reduction is the only lever large enough to move the needle.
The adviser should model the revenue per employee metric to confirm
whether the team is undersized for the work or oversized for the revenue.
