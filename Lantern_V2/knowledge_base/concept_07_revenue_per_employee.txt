METRIC: Revenue per Employee
CATEGORY: Efficiency

DEFINITION
Revenue per employee measures how much revenue the company generates
for each person on its team. In professional services, people are
both the primary cost and the primary value creator — the ratio
between what each employee costs and what each employee generates
is the fundamental unit economics of the business. A low revenue
per employee means the company is either undercharging for its
services, overstaffed, or both.

HOW IT'S CALCULATED
Revenue per Employee = Total Revenue / Number of Active Employees

Active employees = those currently employed (end_date IS NULL)
Use paid revenue only — recognized, collected income.

Annual figure is more meaningful than monthly for services firms
due to billing lumpiness.

BENCHMARKS — PROFESSIONAL SERVICES INDUSTRY
Revenue per Employee (Annual):
  Excellent:  Above $200,000
  Strong:     $150,000 to $200,000
  Adequate:   $100,000 to $150,000
  Weak:       $75,000 to $100,000
  Critical:   Below $75,000

Note: Boutique consulting and high-value advisory firms often
target $250,000 to $400,000 per employee. Technology-enabled
services firms may reach $300,000+. Staffing and lower-margin
services firms typically sit at $75,000 to $120,000.

WHAT IT TELLS THE ADVISER
- Whether the company is generating enough revenue per person
  to sustain healthy margins
- Whether pricing is appropriate for the team's skill level
- Whether the company is overstaffed relative to its revenue base
- How the company compares to industry peers on workforce efficiency

WARNING SIGNS
- Revenue per employee below $100,000 annually in a firm where
  average salary exceeds $70,000 (margins are mathematically impossible)
- Revenue per employee declining over time even while headcount
  is stable (clients are spending less per engagement)
- Large gap between billing rates and revenue per employee suggests
  high unbillable time, scope creep, or poor utilization tracking
- Revenue per employee well below industry benchmarks suggests
  the firm is competing on price rather than value

CONNECTIONS TO OTHER METRICS
- Low revenue per employee + high compensation % of expenses =
  the workforce cost is not justified by what it generates
- Low revenue per employee + strong net margin = unusual;
  likely means very low salaries or a lean operating model
- Low revenue per employee + declining revenue trend = workforce
  was sized for a larger business that no longer exists
- High revenue per employee + high churn rate = a small number
  of high-performing employees carrying the business; fragile

SAMPLE INTERPRETATION
A services firm has 12 active employees and annual paid revenue of
$1,440,000. Revenue per employee is $120,000 — in the Adequate range.
However, if the firm's average fully-loaded employee cost (salary +
benefits + overhead) is $95,000, the margin contribution per employee
is only $25,000 — thin but viable. The adviser should note that any
revenue decline without a corresponding headcount reduction will
push this into Weak territory rapidly. Cross-reference with expense
breakdown to confirm actual compensation costs per head.
