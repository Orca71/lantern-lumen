METRIC: Client Churn Rate
CATEGORY: Risk

DEFINITION
Client churn rate measures the proportion of clients who have stopped
doing business with the company over a given period. In professional
services, churn is the mirror image of retention — and retention is
everything. Acquiring a new client costs five to seven times more than
retaining an existing one. A high churn rate means the company is
running on a leaky bucket: no matter how much new business it wins,
it cannot grow because it keeps losing what it already has.

HOW IT'S CALCULATED
Churn Rate = (Clients Lost in Period / Clients at Start of Period) × 100

A client is considered churned if any of the following apply:
  1. Status field is not 'active'
  2. No invoice activity in the past 90 days (inactive signal)
  3. No invoices ever recorded (acquired but never billed)

Annual churn rate is the standard reporting period.

BENCHMARKS — PROFESSIONAL SERVICES INDUSTRY
Annual Client Churn Rate:
  Excellent:   Under 5%
  Healthy:     5% to 10%
  Acceptable:  10% to 15%
  Warning:     15% to 25%
  Critical:    Above 25%

Retention rate = 100% minus churn rate.
A 20% churn rate means the company must replace one in five clients
every year just to stay flat.

WHAT IT TELLS THE ADVISER
- Whether the company delivers enough value to retain clients
- Whether the sales team is winning new clients to offset losses
- Whether specific client segments or service types have higher attrition
- Whether the revenue base is stable or eroding underneath the surface

WARNING SIGNS
- Churn above 20% annually — the company cannot grow under these conditions
- Churn accelerating year over year (retention is getting worse)
- High churn concentrated in the largest clients (revenue impact
  is larger than the client count suggests)
- Long-tenured clients churning — indicates a change in service quality
  or value proposition, not just natural attrition
- Zero churn — may indicate the client base is very small or that
  inactive clients have not been properly classified

CONNECTIONS TO OTHER METRICS
- High churn + high client concentration = the clients the company
  most depends on are the ones it most needs to watch
- High churn + declining revenue = churn is likely driving the
  revenue decline, not just coinciding with it
- High churn + low DSO = clients are leaving but at least they
  paid before they left (better than the reverse)
- High churn + low net profit margin = the company is not profitable
  enough to invest in the retention activities that would reduce churn
- Low churn + declining revenue = clients are staying but spending less;
  the problem is scope reduction, not relationship loss

SAMPLE INTERPRETATION
A services firm shows 3 out of 11 clients classified as churned — a
27% churn rate. This is Critical. The company is losing more than one
in four client relationships annually. The adviser should investigate
whether the churn is concentrated among smaller clients (less damaging)
or larger ones (potentially catastrophic). Cross-reference with
revenue trend — if revenue is also declining, churn is almost certainly
the primary driver. Immediate strategic focus: implement a formal
client retention program, assign senior leadership to at-risk accounts,
and conduct exit interviews to understand root causes.
