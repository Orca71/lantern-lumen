METRIC: Days Sales Outstanding (DSO)
CATEGORY: Efficiency

DEFINITION
Days Sales Outstanding measures how long, on average, it takes a company
to collect payment after an invoice is issued. It is the gap between
earning revenue and actually receiving the cash. In professional services,
DSO is critical because the primary asset is time — and unbilled or
uncollected time is value that has been delivered but not captured.
High DSO is often the hidden cause of cash flow problems even in
technically profitable companies.

Key terms: accounts receivable, invoice collection, days outstanding,
payment speed, receivables turnover, how fast clients pay, collection
period, billing cycle, overdue invoices, payment delays.

HOW IT'S CALCULATED
DSO = (Accounts Receivable / Total Revenue) × Number of Days in Period

Simplified version:
DSO = Average days between invoice issue_date and paid_date
      for all paid invoices in the period

A DSO of 45 means the company waits 45 days on average to get paid
after sending an invoice.

BENCHMARKS — PROFESSIONAL SERVICES INDUSTRY
Excellent:  Under 30 days
Healthy:    30 to 45 days
Adequate:   45 to 60 days
Warning:    60 to 90 days
Critical:   Above 90 days

WHAT IT TELLS THE ADVISER
- How effectively the company manages its billing and collections process
- Whether clients are paying on agreed terms or stretching payments
- Whether the company has a cash flow problem disguised as a
  profitability problem
- How much working capital is trapped in unpaid invoices

WARNING SIGNS
- DSO above 60 days in a firm with net 30 payment terms
  (clients are routinely paying late — a collections process problem)
- DSO increasing over time even while revenue is stable
  (client payment behavior is deteriorating)
- DSO above 90 days — some invoices may be uncollectable
- Large gap between DSO and standard payment terms suggests
  clients are using the company as an informal credit facility
- High DSO combined with low cash balance is a liquidity crisis
  in slow motion

CONNECTIONS TO OTHER METRICS
- High DSO + low cash balance = immediate cash flow emergency
- High DSO + high client concentration = one slow-paying client
  is distorting the entire metric
- High DSO + declining revenue = clients reducing spend AND
  paying late — relationship deterioration signal
- High DSO + healthy profit margin = profitable on paper,
  struggling in practice

SAMPLE INTERPRETATION
A services firm reports DSO of 84 days against standard net 30 payment
terms. This is in the Critical range and represents a serious operational
failure. Clients are paying on average 54 days late. The adviser should
flag this as a collections process breakdown, not just a client behavior
issue. Immediate actions: review aging receivables by client, identify
the worst offenders, assess whether any receivables are at risk of being
written off. Cross-reference with client concentration — if the
slow-paying clients are also the largest, the risk is compounded.
